Spread across 6 cities and 8 buzzing venues:

To understand what really happened inside these rooms ‚Äî the problem-solving, the frustration, the joy, the unexpected logic leaps ‚Äî we gathered insights from people who lived it from the inside:

  • the kids, whose unfiltered minds created everything from climbing-wall games to fight games,
  • the volunteers, who spent the day helping translate imagination into prompts,
  • and the organisers, who coordinated the chaos across all venues.

This hackathon was not about winners. Instead, it was an experiment in pure creativity ‚Äî a space where kids could take an idea, describe it in their own words, push an AI to its limits, and watch their concepts transform into digital prototypes.

In this article, we explore:

What Is a Kids‚Äô AI Vibe Coding Hackathon, Really?

A kids‚Äô vibe coding hackathon is a hands-on AI creativity challenge where children turn their ideas into working digital prototypes using nothing but natural language. They prompt what they imagined into a tool that turns their prompt into code and displays live the outcome of the code.

Instead of learning coding syntax, they learn something far more foundational: how to think logically, articulate intentions clearly, break problems into steps, and iterate when things don‚Äôt work as expected.

It‚Äôs fast, playful, and deeply empowering, giving every child ‚Äî regardless of background or prior experience ‚Äî an accessible entry point into digital creation.

What Kids Actually Do When They Vibe Code

Children don‚Äôt wait for instructions ‚Äî they build what excites them. At this hackathon, that meant climbing-wall games, simple fight games, rapid small prototypes, and a surprisingly sophisticated fashion-matching app. Vibe coding shows kids that their passions have creative power. What they love becomes what they build, and what they build becomes their first glimpse of how personal interests can generate real value.

What This Teaches Us About Creativity and Learning

Vibe coding gives us a glimpse of how children learn when technology finally bends to their way of thinking. Understanding these new learning pathways might end up reshaping how we think about education altogether.

Interest-Driven Learning that Breaks Disciplines

Kids built what they cared about ‚Äî and their personal interests became the drivers of interdisciplinary learning:

  • The student who designed a climbing-wall game struggled with aesthetics. She spent time choosing colours, movement, and shapes, and in doing so expanded her vocabulary around design, motion, and visual logic.
  • The kid who developed a fighting game realised that if each player has three lives, there must be a counter keeping track of them. A simple idea, but it introduced him to basic game logic and rule-building.
  • The student who created a Minecraft-style world with animals discovered that progression requires resources. He built an inventory system ‚Äî pigs dropping meat, raccoons dropping fur, and so on ‚Äî learning early systems thinking through his own idea.
Working With Constraints

Kids also learned that their ideas don‚Äôt exist in unlimited space ‚Äî and that constraints shape how a game works:

The student building the animal world kept expanding the size of his map whenever he ran out of space. At some point the world became so large that the game slowed down. He realised he had to make a choice: a huge world that lagged, or a smaller world that ran smoothly. This simple moment ‚Äî deciding between space and performance ‚Äî introduced him to trade-offs, optimisation, and the idea that design decisions always come with consequences.

Collaborative Learning

Even though each child worked on their own idea, the learning was strongly social:

Kids leaned over to help each other debug prompts. They compared outputs, asked ‚ÄúHow did you do that?‚Äù, and borrowed ideas when something caught their eye. Small discoveries spread quickly across the room ‚Äî one child figuring something out often meant five others trying it a minute later.

Charleroi chapter was the best example of this - natural communities formed and kids started helping each other.

What started as individual projects became a shared problem-solving environment, where collaboration naturally amplified creativity.

AI as a thinking partner

One of the most fascinating lessons was how children began treating AI as a collaborator rather than a tool. They tested prompts, argued with outputs (‚ÄúNo, not like that‚Äù), refined instructions, and tried again. This back-and-forth dialogue teaches a fundamental 21st-century skill: learning how to think with machines, not just about machines. It‚Äôs a new cognitive partnership ‚Äî accessible even to an 8-year-old.

Empowering Agency

Watching kids vibe code reveals how quickly confidence emerges when children realise they can build something real. The moment they see their idea appear on-screen ‚Äî even imperfectly ‚Äî they shift from users of technology to authors of it.

This shows them that they have agency, ideas can be acted upon, and that technology is something they can shape. Learning becomes self-driven because the motivation is intrinsic.

Problem Solving through play

A kids‚Äô vibe coding hackathon reveals something powerful about how children learn: when the pressure of correctness is removed, their natural cognitive strengths surface. They break problems into steps without being asked, test ideas through trial and error, refine what doesn‚Äôt work, and explain their thinking out loud. These are the foundations of computational thinking ‚Äî formed not through lectures, but through play. It shows that creativity isn‚Äôt separate from logic; the two grow together.

The Challenges Behind the Scenes

No large-scale experiment runs perfectly on the first try. These behind-the-scenes challenges showed us where we can grow, and they will directly inform how we design Vibe Coding 2.0.

Logistics & Tool Setup

Because this hackathon spanned multiple cities on a tight timeline, some tool setup happened on the morning of the event, which created small delays. It showed us that having earlier onboarding and a short volunteer refresher session would make future editions even smoother.

Language Barriers

Since the event relied heavily on expat volunteers who primarily work in English, native-speaking support was uneven across venues. Fortunately, most Belgian students have strong working English skills and adapted well ‚Äî but balanced language coverage will be a priority moving forward.

Recruiting Kids (Leuven Case)

Outreach through school secretaries did not yield results, leading to low sign-ups despite significant effort. Direct engagement with teachers proved far more effective, shaping how we will approach local recruitment next time.

Tool Limitations & Unexpected Outputs

We used Lovable and Rosebud, two tools widely recognised in the vibe coding community and considered among the strongest benchmarks for fast prototyping with AI.

Lovable ‚Äî which excels at structured website/app generation ‚Äî sometimes required a bit more support during ideation, simply because many kids gravitated towards designing games, which is outside Lovable‚Äôs main purpose.

Rosebud enabled children to create impressively diverse, imaginative game assets, and the overwhelming majority of outputs were high-quality and fully appropriate. There was, however, a single instance where the model produced an image that could be viewed as sensitive by stricter standards ‚Äî a reminder of why live supervision and filters are essential when working with minors.

Device Availability

In some venues, organisers initially worried about not having enough laptops for all children. Many families brought their own devices, which solved the issue this time, but future editions will include more explicit planning around equipment availability.

Why These Hackathons Matter

What we saw in these rooms explains why vibe coding is more than a fun activity: it has real implications for parents, schools, and AI literacy.

For Schools

Updating your digital transformation strategy does not necessarily mean finding the shiniest Learning Management System. Sometimes understanding the tools out there can help you update your pedagogy or even solve long-lasting challenges like integrating interdisciplinary learning.

Remember: future skills are not ‚Äúfuture‚Äù anymore.

For Parents

Raising children in the unexplored era of AI can be terrifying. But one does not have to be an AI expert to know how to constructively use the tools for their child's upbringing.

Understanding how new tools can support your child's development is the first step towards finding new activities together, guiding them in processing their interests in an empowering and healthy way, and puts you back in the driver's seat without having to be an AI expert.

For the Future of AI Literacy

According to the OECD‚Äôs AI literacy framework, citizens need to be able to engage with AI, create with AI, manage AI, and eventually design AI. A vibe coding experience gives children an early and meaningful entry point into the first two.

By prompting, testing, and refining their ideas, they learn to engage with AI confidently ‚Äî not as passive users, but as active participants who understand how to communicate with a system. And by turning natural language instructions into working prototypes, they begin to create with AI, experiencing firsthand how their ideas translate into digital form.

These sessions also get them acquainted with the strengths and limitations of current AI tools. Kids quickly notice when systems break, lag, or misinterpret their intent, which makes it easier for them to understand the constraints and design choices behind existing platforms. This is the foundation of design thinking ‚Äî a skill that will become increasingly important as AI continues shaping the tools they use.

What‚Äôs Next for Kids‚Äô Hackathons?

Vibe Coding 2.0 will be bigger and better. Scheduled for Q2 2026, it will build directly on the learnings from this first edition. The aim is to expand into more cities, strengthen local volunteer networks, and introduce a new layer of impact:

systematically tracking the prompts and creations of participants as anonymised data to support research in educational sciences.
